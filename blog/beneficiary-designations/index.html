What Is a Beneficiary Designation, and Why Check Yours?

Jul 16, 2026

Somewhere in a filing cabinet, or a database you have not logged into for years, there is a form with a name on it. You probably filled it in on your first week at a job. That form usually decides where some of the biggest money you will ever leave behind actually goes.

What it actually is

When you opened your 401(k), your IRA or a life insurance policy, you were asked to name a beneficiary: the person the money should be paid to if you die. That designation lives with the plan or the insurer, not with your will. In general, the account is paid to the person named on that form regardless of what your will says.

For workplace retirement plans there is an extra layer of federal law: in most 401(k)-type plans, your spouse automatically has the right to that money unless they have signed a formal written waiver. Naming someone else without that waiver generally does not work.

Why it is worth checking today

These forms are the classic set-and-forget. People name a parent at 25, a first spouse at 30, and never look again. Life moves. The form does not.

Divorce is the sharpest example. For many workplace plans, federal law means the plan pays whoever is on the form, even if that is an ex-spouse and even if the divorce settlement said otherwise. For IRAs and individual life insurance, some states cancel an ex-spouse's designation automatically and others do not. The safe move is the same everywhere: after any big life change, update the forms.

One more trap: naming your estate as beneficiary usually pulls that money back into the probate process, which can slow everything down.

A two-minute job, and a place to record it

Log in to each retirement account and insurance policy, look at who is named, and fix anything that is out of date. Then write down where each account is and who the beneficiary is, somewhere your family can actually find. That is exactly what The Estate Organizer gives you a page for.
