What Does an Executor Actually Do?

Jul 16, 2026

Executor sounds like a title from a courtroom drama. In real life it is quieter and heavier: it is the person who has to finish someone's paperwork after they die.

The short version

The core of the job is the same everywhere: collect everything the person owned, pay their bills and debts, and distribute what remains to the people entitled to it. Many states call this person the personal representative; executor usually means the person named in a will to do it.

What the job often looks like, step by step

Get certified copies of the death certificate. Find the will. Work out whether the estate needs to go through the probate court, which depends on what was owned, how it was titled, and your state's rules. Then build the inventory: bank accounts, retirement accounts, insurance, property, debts. Notify banks and agencies. Pay valid debts. Keep records of everything. Distribute what remains, and account for it.

Some of the biggest items may never pass through the executor's hands at all: retirement accounts and life insurance generally go straight to the beneficiary named on their own forms.

What tends to catch families out

It is rarely the legal steps, it is the finding. Nobody knows how many accounts exist, where the insurance policy is, or what the phone passcode was. The role also carries real responsibility: an executor handles other people's money, must keep proper records, and mistakes can create personal exposure in some situations. Slow and documented beats fast and sorry.

Common questions about being an executor

How long does it take?

It varies widely with the estate and the state, from months for simple estates to considerably longer for complex ones. Beneficiary-designated assets often move faster because they sit outside the court process.

Do I get paid?

Many states allow reasonable compensation for the personal representative, and the will itself can address it. Check how it works where you are.

What order do things get paid in?

Debts and expenses are generally settled before anything is distributed, and the exact priority order is set by state law. When in doubt, get advice before paying anyone.
