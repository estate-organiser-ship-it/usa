Power of Attorney vs a Will: What Is the Difference?

Jul 16, 2026

These two documents get mixed up constantly, and the confusion matters, because each one covers a gap the other cannot.

A will is for after you die

A will says who should receive what you own, and who should be in charge of making that happen. It has no power at all while you are alive, and it only speaks after death.

A power of attorney is for while you are alive

A power of attorney is a legal document that lets someone you choose act on your behalf, for example with banking or bills. Crucially, it works the other way round from a will: it operates during your lifetime and its authority ends at your death.

A standard power of attorney can stop being effective if you become incapacitated, which is exactly when your family needs it most. That is why many people use a durable power of attorney, which stays effective even if you can no longer make decisions yourself. The rules and forms are set by each state.

Health decisions are a separate piece again. A living will records the treatment you would and would not want, and a durable power of attorney for health care names a person, sometimes called a health care proxy, to make medical decisions for you if you cannot. Names for these documents vary by state.

Why the gap matters

Picture the in-between: someone is alive but cannot manage their own affairs after a stroke or a diagnosis. The will cannot help, it is not in force yet. Without the right power of attorney documents, even a spouse can hit walls trying to deal with banks or make care decisions, and a court process may be the only path.

What to do about it

Getting these documents done properly is a job for an attorney or a reputable legal service in your state. And once they exist, they only help if people can find them. Record what exists, where it is kept and who holds which role in one place your family knows about. That is a core chapter of The Estate Organizer.
